Introduction

Who is the audience?


Scholars Interest
Organization theory Social evaluations of organizational identities (Cudennec & Durand 2023)
Institutional theory Collective vs. individual identity in markets (Banerjee et al. 2022)
Strategic management Balance between legitimacy and distinctiveness (Barlow et al. 2019)
Sociology of organizations Visual dimension of organizational identity (Boxembaum et al. 2018)

What are the outcomes?


  • Producer performance (e.g. Bu et al. 2022; Haans 2019)
    • Sales
    • Stock performance
    • Downloads
    • Survival
    • Investment
  • Audience appeal (e.g. Verhaal et al. 2015)
    • Ratings
    • Review content
    • Cognitive processing
    • Consensus

What's the current understanding?

  • Audiences expect certain prototypical features (e.g. Kovács & Johnson 2014)
  • Limited resources mean audiences engage in 2-stage selection process (Phillips & Zuckerman 2001)
    • Stage 1: Assemble a subset of legitimate offerings
    • Stage 2: Choose the most novel offering in the set
  • Producers must conform for legitimacy, differentiate for novelty (see Zhao 2022 for a review)
  • Trade-off between being boring (heavy competition) vs. confusing (overlooked)

What's the gap?

  • Typically researchers have relied on verbal texts to study the selection process
  • Distinctiveness research has incorporated visuals in 2 main ways:
    1. Substitute visuals for verbal in empirical analyses, assume the mechanism is the same (e.g. Banerjee et al. 2023; Bu et al. 2022)
    2. Theorize how producers combine visual & verbal modes, treat them as interchangeable (e.g. Forgues & May 2017; Zamparini & Lurati 2017)

What's new?


  • We know individuals interpret visual and verbal texts in very different ways (Jewitt 2014; Kress & van Leeuwen 2020)
    • Visual: Immediate, holistic, emotional, fast
    • Verbal: Linear, cumulative, analytical, slow
  • We know relatively little about how audience perceptions of distinctiveness depend on the mode through which features are communicated

What research questions are we asking?


  1. Is APPEAL=f(DISTINCTIVENESS) the same for visual vs. verbal modes?
  2. How do modes affect consideration in stage 1 and selection in stage 2?
  3. How do audiences interpret distinctiveness through visual and verbal modes?
  4. Are the legitimacy penalties different when distinctiveness is communicated visually vs. verbally?
  5. How does the communication of distinctiveness in one mode depend on the other?

What is the motivation behind this paper?


  • The visual mode is increasingly important for organizations
  • We assume that identity is communicated the same visually and verbally
  • This ignores possibility of different legitimacy penalties for modes
  • Fails to account for more optimal identity by combining verbal and visual
Theory

Category schemas and features


  • Audiences expect category members to possess certain important features
    • Category schema
  • Org. identity is communicated through positioning, products, narratives
  • Distincitveness is how different a producer's features are from competitors
    • Category prototype
  • Producers with more similar features are seen as more legitimate

    • (see Bitektine & Haack 2015)

Cognitive ambiguity and processing


  • Distinctive identities lead to cognitive ambiguity
  • Ambiguous identities require more effort to evaluate
  • Default processing is fast/automatic
  • Audiences skip ambiguous offerings, more likely to consier clearer ones

    • (see Hannan et al. 2019)

Verbal vs. visual texts


Verbal Visual
Interpreted in a slow, active way Interpreted in a fast, passive way
Linear, additive Holistic, immediate
Rational language, relationships between features Captures attention through emotions, affect
More shared conventions b/c syntax, grammar Individual interpretation varies more
(see Meyer et al. 2013, 2018) (see Höllerer et al. 2013, 2018)

Hypotheses


  1. There is an inverted U-shaped relationship between the typicality of text in restaurants’ online menus and performance

  2. There is a positive relationship between the typicality of images in restaurants’ menus and performance

  3. Image typicality negatively moderates the relationship between text typicality and performance
Data and Methods

Empirical context


  • Data scraped from 8000+ restaurants on foodpanda.com
  • Sampled from 6 major cities across Taiwan
  • Collected banner images, menu items, reviews, metadata
  • Separated into 12 competitive groups based on cuisine categories
    • Taiwanese, Japanese, European, Western, etc.

Overview of studies

  1. Correlational study
    • Analyzed reviews and menus from 4,208 restaurants
    • Negative binomial regression models predicting performance
  2. Experimental study
    • Created online experiment using menus from Study 1

Study 1 - variables

Type Construct Measure
Dependent Performance Number of reviews gained in one year
Independent Verbal typicality Mean similarity to competitors
Independent Visual typicality Mean similarity to competitors
Control Quality Food ratings from users
Control Experience Number of months on platform
Control Price level Price category ($-$$$)
Control Cuisine Categorical dummies for primary cuisine
Control Demographics Categorical dummies for city

Study 1 - methodology


  • Dependent Variable

    1. Accessed foodpanda internal API to get exact number of reviews
    2. Calculated difference in number of reviews from time-0 to time-1

  • Independent Variables

    1. Used deep learning methodology (text embeddings) to vectorize text and images
    2. Calculated cosine similarity between each restaurant and all competitors in the same cuisine category
    3. Used mean of all distances

Study 1 - models


  • Used negative binomial regression models

    • A general linear model similar to Poisson regression
    • Uses a count of the number an event occurs as the dependent variable
    • Used in cases where DV is over-dispersed
    • Does not assume mean and variance of DV are the same

  • Added squared terms to models to check for U-shaped relationships

Study 2 - Sample

  • 334 unique responses from NSYSU students
  • 53% were graduate students
  • 39% were male
  • 80% were between 18-24 years of age
  • Median completion time for the experiment was 297 seconds

Study 2 - Experimental manipulations

  • DV: level of interest in 5 Japanese restaurants (9 pt. likert-type)
  • Between-subjects: Mode (text-only, images-only)
  • Within-subjects: Typicality (very low, low, moderate, high, very high)
  • Manipulation check: Typicality of Japanese restaurants (9 pt. likert-type)

Conclusion

This paper:

  • Incorporates ideas from research into multimodality, social semiotics and organization theory
  • Investigates how audiences interpret multimodal identity claims.
  • Shows that appeal is highest if distinctiveness is moderate for verbal, low for visual
  • Suggests that verbal and visual identity claims are not merely interchangeable/additive

Contributes to theory on:

  • Optimal distinctiveness
  • The material and visual components of organizational identity
  • Strategic positioning in online platforms
  • Social evaluations of organizations
